Understanding Virginia's Snakes:
Facts and Identification

By SaveSnakes.org Sep 5, 2025 6 min read Updated Sep 7, 2025

Virginia is home to a diverse range of wildlife, and snakes are among the most fascinating creatures you can encounter. Whether you are a nature enthusiast, a hiker, or simply curious about the local fauna, understanding Virginia's snakes can enhance your outdoor experiences. Virginia boasts around 30 species of snakes, with a mix of both venomous and non-venomous varieties. Knowing how to identify these snakes can help you appreciate their role in the ecosystem and ensure your safety during outdoor activities.

Venomous Snakes in Virginia

There are only 3 venomous snakes in Virginia. These pit viper species have a heat-sensing pit located between the eye and nostril - used to aid in prey location and allow prey capture in dark spaces, like rodent burrows.

⚠️ Virginia's Venomous Species

  • Eastern Copperhead - Most common venomous snake in Virginia.
  • Northern Cottonmouth (Water Moccasin) - Found near Virginia Beach area (South East Virginia).
  • Timber Rattlesnake (Cane Brake) - Found in North Eastern Virginia on mountains and some areas near South West Virginia.

Eastern Copperhead

Eastern Copperhead ⚠ Venomous
Head:  Coppery-red, often triangular shaped
Body:  Hourglass-shaped bands (like Hershey's Kisses or an hourglass) - narrow near backbone, wide on sides
Color:  Adults are patterned; juveniles have sulfur-yellow tail tips
Range:  Found almost all of Virginia
⚠ Often confused with: Water Snakes, Corn Snakes, and Juvenile Eastern Ratsnakes. Key difference - copperheads have hourglass bands and coppery-orange triangular heads; juveniles have bright yellow tail tips.

Northern Cottonmouth (Water Moccasin)

Northern Cottonmouth ⚠ Venomous
Head:  Dark mask above the eye - "Mask of Zorro!"
Body:  Dark, thick-bodied with Christmas Tree / Dumbbell pattern (broader at bottom, narrow on top). Some appear dark with no pattern.
Size:  Up to 50-60 inches; usually thick and large-bodied
Prey:  Fish, reptiles, amphibians (frogs), small mammals, invertebrates
Juveniles have a sulfur-yellow tail tip and brighter pattern colors.
⚠ Often confused with: The harmless Northern Water Snake. Cottonmouths have thick blocky heads; water snakes have dark lines on the edge of each lip scale.

Timber Rattlesnake (Canebrake)

Timber Rattlesnake ⚠ Venomous
Body:  Highly variable; most have zigzag-shaped blotches (chevrons) and crossbands
Size:  Up to 6 feet in length
Range:  North Eastern Virginia mountains, some areas near South West Virginia
Prey:  Squirrels, rats, mice, cottontail rabbits, skinks, birds
🎶 Fun fact: The rattle is made of special hollow scales of keratin on the tip of the tail. When the rattlesnake vibrates its tail, the loosely connected scales rapidly strike each other, creating that distinctive buzzing sound.

Non-Venomous Snakes in Virginia

Virginia has many non-venomous snakes. Let's learn about the most common ones seen in our neighborhoods. Remember: Even if you can identify a snake, leave it to go on its way! Never handle any snakes if possible. If bitten by a non-venomous snake, just wash the wound with soap and water.

🐍 The 5 Most Common Non-Venomous Snakes

  • Eastern Rat Snake (aka Black Rat Snake)
  • Eastern Garter Snake
  • Dekay's Brown Snake
  • Eastern Kingsnake
  • Northern Water Snake

Eastern Rat Snake (Black Rat Snake)

🐍 Eastern Rat Snake (Black Rat Snake) ✓ Non-Venomous
Appearance:  Long (up to 6 feet), shiny black with white chin and belly
Juveniles:  Gray with darker brown blotches - looks patterned; adults are solid shiny black
Where:  Climbing trees, attics, barns, or near wood piles
  
🌟 Fun fact: Great climbers and help control rodents!
⚠ Misidentified as: Juvenile Copperhead. Key difference - copperheads have hourglass bands, coppery triangular heads, and bright yellow tail tips. Rat snakes don't have yellow tail tips.

Eastern Garter Snake

🐍 Eastern Garter Snake ✓ Non-Venomous
Appearance:  Thin, 1.5 to 3 feet long, olive, brown, or black with yellowish stripes down the back
Juveniles:  Thinner and more vibrant in color; adult stripes can fade or become duller with age
Where:  Lawns, gardens, parks - often near water
  
🌟 Fun fact: They give birth to live young - not eggs like many other snakes! They can also release a smelly musk if picked up, as a defense.
⚠ Lookalike: Ribbon snakes - more slender, white markings around the eyes, and a longer tail.

Dekay's Brown Snake

🐍 Dekay's Brown Snake ✓ Non-Venomous
Appearance:  Small (8-15 inches), brown or gray with two rows of small dark spots
Juveniles:  Brown or gray with a whitish ring around the neck
Where:  Under rocks, mulch, garden debris
Food:  Slugs, snails, earthworms, soft-bodied insects - great for gardens!
🌟 Fun fact: Very shy, totally harmless, and often mistaken for worms.

Eastern Kingsnake

🐍 Eastern Kingsnake ✓ Non-Venomous
Appearance:  Glossy black with thin white or yellow chain-like bands
Size:  About 3-5 feet long, robust and confident in movement
Where:  Wooded areas, fields, backyards
  
🌟 Fun fact: Immune to venom from copperheads - and actually eats them!
⚠ Lookalike: Black racer or rat snake. Kingsnakes have distinct banding; racers are thinner, faster, and may flee quickly.

Northern Water Snake

🐍 Northern Water Snake ✓ Non-Venomous
Appearance:  Thick-bodied, brown or reddish with darker bands
Juveniles:  Tan or light gray with very clear red or brown bands
Adults:  Can appear dark brown or almost black, especially when wet
Where:  Near creeks, ponds, or rivers
🌟 Fun fact: They are very protective and can bite when picked up, but are totally non-venomous.
⚠ Often mistaken for: Cottonmouth (Water Moccasin). How to tell: Cottonmouths have thick blocky heads; Water Snakes have dark lines on the edge of each labial (lip) scale.

How to Identify Snakes

Identifying snakes can be challenging, but there are key features to look for. Here is a quick reference guide:

⚠️ Identification Caveats

  • Head shape: Triangular heads are sometimes said to indicate venomous snakes, but this is NOT conclusive - do not rely on this alone.
  • Eye pupils: Vertical pupils are sometimes associated with venomous snakes in the US, but this is NOT 100% guaranteed.
  • Color patterns are the most reliable field guide, but juveniles can look different from adults.
  • Non-venomous snakes in the USA often have vertical dark bars on the jaw.

Safety Tips When Encountering Snakes

✅ What to Do If You See a Snake

  • Stay Calm: Most snakes are not aggressive and will not attack unless provoked.
  • Keep Your Distance: Give it space. Do not attempt to touch or capture it.
  • Observe from Afar: Take a moment to observe from a safe distance to help identify the species.
  • Never Handle: Even non-venomous snakes can bite. Observe, don't grab.
  • Educate Yourself: Familiarize yourself with local snakes - knowledge is your best safety tool.
  • Call for Help: If a snake is inside your home or property, call SaveSnakes.org for free removal.

The Role of Snakes in the Ecosystem

Snakes play a vital role in maintaining the balance of ecosystems. They help control populations of rodents and other small animals, which can prevent overpopulation and the spread of disease. Additionally, snakes serve as prey for larger animals, contributing to the food chain.

Understanding their role can help foster a greater appreciation for these often-misunderstood creatures. A world with healthy snake populations is a world with balanced, thriving ecosystems.

Embracing Virginia's Serpentine Residents

Virginia's snakes are an integral part of the state's natural heritage. By learning about their characteristics, habitats, and behaviors, you can enhance your outdoor experiences and contribute to the conservation of these fascinating reptiles.

Next time you venture into the wild, take a moment to appreciate the snakes you encounter. Whether they are slithering through the grass or basking in the sun, these creatures are a testament to the beauty and diversity of Virginia's wildlife.
