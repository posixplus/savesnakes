Get to Know Your Neighbors

Meet Virginia's Snakes

Most snakes you'll meet in Virginia are completely harmless and quietly helpful. Get to know a few of the locals - and the handful you'll want to admire from a distance.

Harmless

Eastern Garter Snake

Thamnophis sirtalis
Appearance
Slender, 1.5 to 3 ft; olive, brown or black with three pale yellow stripes.
Where you'll see them
Yards, gardens & parks
Lookalike
Ribbon snake, which is more slender with white markings near the eyes.
Virginia's most common backyard snake. Completely harmless - it eats slugs, worms and frogs, and gives birth to live young.
Harmless

Eastern Rat Snake

Pantherophis alleghaniensis
Appearance
Up to 6 ft; glossy black adults with a white chin; juveniles are gray with dark blotches.
Where you'll see them
Trees, barns & wood piles
Lookalike
Juveniles are mistaken for copperheads, but lack hourglass bands and a yellow tail tip.
Virginia's longest snake - up to six feet of glossy black with a white chin. A superb climber and the area's best rodent control.
Harmless

Eastern Kingsnake

Lampropeltis getula
Appearance
Glossy black, 3 to 5 ft, with a pale chain-link pattern of bands.
Where you'll see them
Woods, fields & backyards
Lookalike
Black racer or rat snake, but only the kingsnake has the chain banding.
Glossy black with a pale chain-link pattern. Famously immune to copperhead venom - and it actually eats them.
Harmless

Northern Water Snake

Nerodia sipedon
Appearance
Thick-bodied, brown or reddish with darker bands; looks almost black when wet.
Where you'll see them
Rivers, ponds & creeks
Lookalike
Cottonmouth, but water snakes have a narrow head and dark bars on each lip scale.
Constantly mistaken for a cottonmouth, but harmless. A strong swimmer that keeps fish and frog populations in balance.
Harmless

DeKay's Brownsnake

Storeria dekayi
Appearance
Tiny, 8 to 15 in; brown or gray with two rows of small dark spots.
Where you'll see them
Gardens & leaf litter
Lookalike
Often mistaken for an earthworm or a juvenile of another species.
A tiny, gentle snake rarely longer than a pencil - a gardener's best friend that feeds on slugs and snails.
Venomous

Eastern Copperhead

Agkistrodon contortrix
Appearance
Coppery, triangular head with dark hourglass bands; juveniles have a yellow tail tip.
Where you'll see them
Forest edges & rocky areas
Lookalike
Harmless water, corn and juvenile rat snakes are often blamed for it.
Venomous. Virginia's most common venomous snake, with hourglass bands and a copper head. Bites are rarely fatal - give it room.
Venomous

Northern Cottonmouth

Agkistrodon piscivorus
Appearance
Dark, thick-bodied pit viper with a blocky head and a dark eye mask.
Where you'll see them
Swamps in far SE Virginia
Lookalike
The harmless Northern Water Snake, but cottonmouths are bulkier with a blockier head.
Venomous. A semi-aquatic pit viper that gapes to flash its white 'cotton' mouth when threatened. Rare across most of Virginia.
Venomous

Timber Rattlesnake

Crotalus horridus
Appearance
Heavy-bodied, up to 6 ft, with black chevron bands and a rattle on the tail.
Where you'll see them
Mountains & deep forest
Lookalike
Hard to mistake up close; listen for the warning rattle and never approach.
Venomous. A shy, well-camouflaged forest dweller that rattles a clear warning long before it ever strikes.

Stay Safe, Stay Kind

Tell them apart

Identifying snakes can be tricky - and a harmless look-alike is often blamed for a venomous one. Here's how to read the clues, stay safe, and let snakes do their job.

  1. 1

    The odds are on your side

    Virginia has 30+ snake species and only three are venomous. The snake in your garden is almost certainly harmless.

  2. 2

    Head shape isn't reliable

    Harmless snakes flatten their heads into a triangle to mimic vipers. Don't judge by shape alone.

  3. 3

    Look, don't touch

    Give any snake about six feet of space and it will move on. Most bites happen when people try to handle or kill them.

  4. 4

    When in doubt, send a photo

    We'll identify it for free, usually within the hour - and relocate it if needed.

"If you have to rely on a snake's pupils to identify it, you are already far too close. Leave snakes be."

Did You Know?

Snake myths, busted

A lot of fear comes from a few stubborn myths. Here's the truth - and why the snake next door is more friend than foe.

Myth

"A triangular head means it's venomous."

Many harmless snakes flatten their heads into a triangle when scared. Shape alone is never a reliable clue.

Myth

"Snakes chase people."

They can't, and they won't. A startled snake flees - sometimes toward the nearest cover, which only looks like a chase.

Myth

"Baby venomous snakes are deadlier."

A myth. Neither babies nor adults want to bite you. Give any snake space and it will simply leave.

Myth

"The only good snake is a dead one."

Snakes are free pest control, eating the rodents that spread disease. A healthy snake means a healthier yard.

Myth

"Vertical pupils mean venomous, round pupils are not venomous."

Sometimes light can cause the pupils appear rounded, and can misidentify a venomous snake as non-venomous.
