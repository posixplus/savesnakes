Stay Safe, Stay Kind

Tell them apart

Identifying snakes can be tricky - and a harmless look-alike is often blamed for a venomous one. Here's how to read the clues, stay safe, and let snakes do their job.

  1. 1

    The odds are on your side

    Virginia has 30+ snake species and only three are venomous. The snake in your garden is almost certainly harmless.

  2. 2

    Head shape isn't reliable

    Harmless snakes flatten their heads into a triangle to mimic vipers. Don't judge by shape alone.

  3. 3

    Look, don't touch

    Give any snake about six feet of space and it will move on. Most bites happen when people try to handle or kill them.

  4. 4

    When in doubt, send a photo

    We'll identify it for free, usually within the hour - and relocate it if needed.

"If you have to rely on a snake's pupils to identify it, you are already far too close. Leave snakes be."

Did You Know?

Snake myths, busted

A lot of fear comes from a few stubborn myths. Here's the truth - and why the snake next door is more friend than foe.

Myth

"A triangular head means it's venomous."

Many harmless snakes flatten their heads into a triangle when scared. Shape alone is never a reliable clue.

Myth

"Snakes chase people."

They can't, and they won't. A startled snake flees - sometimes toward the nearest cover, which only looks like a chase.

Myth

"Baby venomous snakes are deadlier."

A myth. Neither babies nor adults want to bite you. Give any snake space and it will simply leave.

Myth

"The only good snake is a dead one."

Snakes are free pest control, eating the rodents that spread disease. A healthy snake means a healthier yard.

Myth

"Vertical pupils mean venomous, round pupils are not venomous."

Sometimes light can cause the pupils appear rounded, and can misidentify a venomous snake as non-venomous.
